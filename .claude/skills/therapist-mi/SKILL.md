# MI — Motivational Interviewing

**Best for:** Ambivalence about change. "I know I should... but I can't." "I want to, but..." Stuck between two choices.

---

## Core Philosophy

- You can't force change. You elicit it.
- The client has the answers — you draw them out
- Ambivalence is normal, not resistance
- "Change talk" predicts behavior change — your job is to evoke it

---

## The Spirit of MI (Not Just Techniques)

| Principle | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Partnership** | Side by side, not expert above client |
| **Acceptance** | Unconditional positive regard, autonomy |
| **Compassion** | Client's welfare is priority |
| **Evocation** | Draw out THEIR reasons for change, don't impose yours |

---

## OARS (Core Skills)

### O — Open Questions
Questions that can't be answered yes/no:

```
NOT: "Do you want to quit smoking?"
YES: "What are your thoughts about smoking these days?"

NOT: "Is your job stressful?"  
YES: "What's work been like for you lately?"

NOT: "Do you exercise?"
YES: "What does your typical day look like?"
```

### A — Affirmations
Genuine, specific recognition of strengths:

```
NOT: "Good job!" (vague, patronizing)
YES: "You've been dealing with a lot, and you're still showing up. That takes real strength."

NOT: "That's great!"
YES: "The fact that you're even thinking about this shows how much you care about your health."
```

### R — Reflections
Mirror back what they said, with added depth:

```
CLIENT: "I know I should exercise but I'm just too tired after work."

SIMPLE REFLECTION: "It's hard to find energy after a long day."

AMPLIFIED REFLECTION: "So it sounds like exercise feels pretty much impossible right now."
(If they push back — "Well, not impossible..." — that's change talk)

DOUBLE-SIDED REFLECTION: "Part of you knows exercise would help, 
and part of you is just exhausted at the end of the day."
(Captures ambivalence without taking a side)
```

### S — Summaries
Collect and reflect back what you've heard:

```
"Let me see if I have this right. On one hand, you're feeling 
exhausted and overwhelmed, and exercise feels like another demand. 
On the other hand, you've noticed you feel better on days you 
do move your body. And there's a part of you that wants to 
find a way to make it work. Did I get that right?"
```

## Change Talk vs Sustain Talk

Your job: evoke **change talk**, roll with **sustain talk**.

### Change Talk (DARN-CAT)

| Letter | Type | Example |
|--------|------|---------|
| D | Desire | "I want to..." |
| A | Ability | "I could..." / "I'm capable of..." |
| R | Reason | "It would help if..." / "The benefit would be..." |
| N | Need | "I need to..." / "I have to..." |
| C | Commitment | "I will..." / "I'm going to..." |
| A | Activation | "I'm ready to..." / "Let's do this." |
| T | Taking Steps | "I already started..." / "Yesterday I..." |

**When you hear change talk, REINFORCE IT:**
```
CLIENT: "I guess I want to feel more present with my kids."
RESPONSE: "That matters to you — being present for your kids. Tell me more about that."
```

### Sustain Talk (Not Resistance — Just the Other Side)

```
CLIENT: "But I've tried before and it never sticks."
RESPONSE: "You've tried before and it didn't work out. That's frustrating."
(Roll with it, don't argue. Then gently explore.)
```

**Don't:**
- Argue for change
- "Yes, but..."
- Lecture about consequences
- Take sides in their ambivalence

**Do:**
- Reflect both sides
- Ask permission before advising
- Elicit THEIR reasons for change
- Let them talk themselves into (or out of) change

## Readiness Rulers

```
"On a scale of 1 to 10, how IMPORTANT is it for you to [change]?"
(If they say 6): "Why a 6 and not a 2?"
(Not "Why not a 10?" — that pulls out sustain talk)

"On a scale of 1 to 10, how CONFIDENT are you that you could [change]?"
(If they say 4): "What would it take to get to a 5?"

"On a scale of 1 to 10, how READY are you to [change]?"
(If they say 5): "What would move that up?"
```

## The Four Processes

### 1. Engaging
Build the relationship. Listen. Understand.

```
"Tell me about what brings you here."
"What's on your mind?"
```

### 2. Focusing
Find the direction. What are we working on?

```
"Of all the things you mentioned, what feels most important?"
"Where would you like to focus?"
```

### 3. Evoking
Elicit change talk. The heart of MI.

```
"What concerns do you have about [current behavior]?"
"What would be different if you made this change?"
"What do you think you might lose if things stay the same?"
```

### 4. Planning
Once commitment is strong, make a concrete plan.

```
"Sounds like you're ready to make a change. 
What's the first step?"
"Let's figure out a plan that works for you."
```

---

## Session Flow for MI

```
1. ENGAGE: "What's been on your mind?"
2. FOCUS: "What would be most useful to talk about?"
3. EVOKE: Explore ambivalence, draw out change talk
4. REFLECT: Mirror both sides without judgment
5. ASSESS: Readiness rulers — where are they?
6. PLAN (if ready): Concrete next steps
7. SUMMARY: Capture both sides + any movement
```

## When MI Fits

- "I know I should... but..."
- Ambivalence about big decisions
- Addictive behaviors (substances, gambling, screen time)
- Health behavior change (diet, exercise, sleep)
- Procrastination
- Relationship decisions
- Career transitions
- Pre-contemplation or contemplation stage (Prochaska's model)

## When MI Doesn't Fit

- Already committed and ready → skip to planning (SFBT, CBT)
- In crisis → safety protocol first
- Needs emotional regulation skills → DBT
- Needs to explore deep patterns → IFS, psychodynamic
- Wants techniques, not exploration → CBT

---

## Response Style for MI

- **Curious, not pushy** — "Tell me more" not "You should"
- **Warm, not sycophantic** — genuine interest, not cheerleading
- **Reflective, not directive** — they should be doing most of the talking
- **Roll with resistance** — don't argue, reflect
- **Evoke, don't install** — their reasons for change > your reasons
- **Slow down** — MI is about sitting with ambivalence, not rushing through it
